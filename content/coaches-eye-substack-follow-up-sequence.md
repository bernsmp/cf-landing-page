```
Quick observation from building The Coach's Eye:

None of the nine leaders in the guide started with a named framework.

Belichick did not wake up and announce the Zoom Lens. Popovich did not brand the Deliberate Mirror. McChrystal did not begin with a tidy little diagram on a whiteboard.

They did the thing for years.

Then the pattern appeared.

Usually, that is how expertise works. The pattern gets repeated so many times that it stops feeling like a pattern. It becomes the hum of the refrigerator. Always there. Easy to stop hearing.

Your work probably has something like this too.

The question you always ask.
The client signal you always catch.
The order you use when everyone else is still sorting the pieces on the table.

The pattern thinks you already know.

You probably do not.

That is why public examples help. You are not inside Belichick's habits or Popovich's standards. You can stand across the room and watch the structure.

Then you bring the same lens back to your own desk.

Try this today:

Ask:

1. What question do I keep asking without naming it?
2. What order do I naturally put things in?
3. What do I notice before anyone else does?

Do not turn it into a framework yet. Just underline the repeat.

That is the weekly work inside the Cognitive Fingerprint Substack. How expertise hides. How patterns become visible. What changes when you can finally name the thing you have been doing all along.
```

## Email 3

Subject: Michael Jordan could feel it

Alternate subjects:

- The expert's worst hiding place
- Instinct is where the receipt got lost

Preview: Feeling it is different from explaining it.

```
There is a line in The Coach's Eye that carries more weight than it looks like:

Michael Jordan did not know what made Michael Jordan great.

He could feel it. The game slowed down. The defender leaned the wrong way. The decision arrived before the explanation.

But feeling a pattern is different from being able to name it.

That gap is where a lot of expert work gets stuck.

A consultant knows which client problem matters first, but calls it instinct. A coach hears the sentence behind the sentence, but calls it experience. A founder knows the right cut to make, but calls it taste.

Those words are not wrong.

They are just foggy.

The useful question is: what would become possible if the fog had labels?

You could teach the move.
You could sell the move.
You could stop rebuilding the move from scratch every time someone asked how you did it.

Here is the Coach's Eye prompt for today:

Think of one thing you are good at but explain badly.

Then finish these sentences:

1. I know it is working when I notice...
2. I know it is going wrong when I notice...
3. The first move I make before I explain myself is...
4. Someone else would probably call this instinct, but I am really tracking...

That last sentence is usually where the name starts.

The Cognitive Fingerprint Substack is where I keep unpacking that fog. I write about the invisible mechanics behind visible expertise, using real examples, public figures, client patterns, and the occasional prompt that got out of hand.

If you liked studying the patterns in other people, this is where we turn the lens toward your own work.
```
